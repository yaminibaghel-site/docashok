Understanding urostomy

A urostomy diverts urine from a diseased bladder to an external pouch — the procedures involved, and what daily life is like.

What is a urostomy?

A urostomy is surgery that diverts urine away from a diseased or defective bladder. The most common forms are ileal and cecal conduit procedures, in which a section of the ileum or cecum becomes a passageway (conduit) carrying urine from the kidneys to a stoma on the abdomen. The diseased bladder may also be removed. In some cases the ureters are brought directly to the abdominal wall, creating a ureterostomy.

Why is a urostomy created?

It addresses defects or problems in the urinary system that require removal or bypass — ensuring urine can be eliminated when the bladder or urinary tract isn't working correctly.

Ileal and cecal conduits

Ileal conduit

A segment of the ileum forms the passageway, diverting urine from the kidneys to an external stoma and bypassing the bladder entirely.

Cecal conduit

A portion of the cecum forms the conduit, similarly directing urine from the kidneys to an external stoma.

Living with a urostomy

Urostomies may be needed from birth (urinary system defects) or in adulthood — for example, neurological disorders causing severe incontinence, or bladder cancer.

Urine flows continuously from a urostomy, so a urostomy bag with a tap is worn and emptied several times a day. The urine may contain some mucus, which is normal. At night, the bag is usually connected to a larger night-drainage bag for continuous collection during sleep.
