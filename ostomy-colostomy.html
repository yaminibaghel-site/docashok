Understanding colostomy

A colostomy brings part of the colon to the abdominal wall to manage waste — here's what it is, why it's created, and what to expect.

What is a colostomy?

A colostomy is a surgically created opening in the colon (large intestine), where the end or side of the colon is brought to the abdominal wall. It can be temporary or permanent, depending on the condition being treated and the part of the colon involved.

Why is a colostomy created?

Colostomies treat various disorders of the large intestine — including cancer, obstruction, inflammatory bowel disease, a ruptured diverticulum, ischaemia, or traumatic injury — and are classified by their location and how long they're needed.

Temporary vs. permanent

Temporary colostomy

Diverts stool away from injured or diseased bowel so it can heal — important after removing diseased colon or rectum, allowing the joined bowel ends (the anastomosis) to recover properly.

Permanent colostomy

Needed when the lower bowel is completely removed or blocked, or the disease is inoperable. It provides a long-term route for waste elimination.

Living with a colostomy

After surgery, the intestines generally work much as before, with a few differences:

  • The colon and rectum beyond the colostomy may be disconnected or removed.
  • The anus is no longer used for stool, but may still pass mucus occasionally — which is normal.

Some people feel urges or notice discharge from the anus after surgery — this can include mucus, and sometimes blood or stool that was present before the operation. If the rectum remains, it continues to make mucus that can be passed harmlessly whenever there's an urge.
