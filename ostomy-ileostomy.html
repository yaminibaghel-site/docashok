Understanding ileostomy

An ileostomy brings the end of the small intestine to the surface, bypassing the colon — the reasons behind it and the road to recovery.

What is an ileostomy?

An ileostomy is a surgically created opening in the ileum — the lower part of the small intestine — brought through the abdominal wall to form a stoma, usually on the lower right side of the abdomen. It may be temporary or permanent and can involve removing all or part of the colon.

Why is an ileostomy created?

An ileostomy provides an alternative route for waste, bypassing the colon and rectum. Whether it's needed — and for how long — depends on each patient's specific condition.

Temporary vs. permanent

Temporary ileostomy

Often recommended during bowel surgery, such as removing a segment of bowel. It lets the intestines heal without the stress of normal digestion, supporting a better recovery.

Permanent ileostomy

Required in more severe cases needing a long-term solution — extensive disease, irreparable damage, or a complete bypass of the colon.

Conditions that may require an ileostomy

Chronic ulcerative colitis

A major disease of the large intestine that may need surgery when it doesn't respond to medical therapy. A common option is total colectomy with an ileal pouch anastomosis and a temporary ileostomy.

Other conditions

Bowel obstruction, cancer of the colon and/or rectum, congenital bowel defects, and injuries to the intestinal tract can also call for an ileostomy to manage symptoms and improve quality of life.
