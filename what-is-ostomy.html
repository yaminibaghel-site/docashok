What is an ostomy?

A surgically created opening in the abdominal wall that reroutes bodily waste — why it is needed, and what living with a stoma involves.

An ostomy (also called a stoma) is a surgically created opening in the abdominal wall that allows fecal matter or urine to leave the body. It becomes necessary for a range of medical reasons — congenital conditions, cancerous or non-cancerous tumours, inflammatory bowel disease, diverticulitis, incontinence, and severe abdominal or pelvic trauma.

Why is an ostomy needed?

The main purpose of an ostomy is to reroute waste from its usual path when part of the digestive or urinary system isn't working as it should. Depending on the underlying condition, this diversion can be either temporary or permanent.

Understanding a stoma

A stoma is the actual opening created during surgery — usually in the colon, ileum or urinary tract — brought to the surface of the abdomen. It typically appears moist and dark pink. For most ostomies, a pouch (collecting bag) is worn over the stoma to gather stool or urine.

A new normal

While an ostomy is vital for health, it brings real changes to body image and daily routine. Adjusting can raise worries about the future and social life — especially where education and support are limited. With the right guidance, ostomates adapt and lead full, active lives.

Continent diversion: an alternative

In some cases, a patient may be eligible for a continent diversion — an alternative that avoids the need for an external pouch and can meaningfully improve quality of life for those who qualify.

The key message: with reliable information, the right appliances and good support, life with an ostomy can be active, social and fulfilling.
This information is for general education and does not replace personalised medical advice. Please consult a qualified surgeon about your specific condition. — Ostomy Care India
